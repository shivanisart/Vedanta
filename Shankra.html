Shankaracharya was an 8th-century Indian philosopher and sage who reshaped Vedanta by teaching Advaita, or non-dualism. He said the ultimate reality, Brahman, is one—unchanging, infinite, and pure consciousness—and our true self, Atman, is identical to it. The world we see, with its forms and changes, is Mithya, not false but impermanent, like a dream that feels real while it lasts. He used the term Trikala Bhititam to describe Sat, the truth that stays constant through past, present, and future, beyond space and objects. Through his commentaries on the Upanishads, like the Mandukya, and his own works, he guided people to self-realization, urging them to see beyond life’s illusions to the oneness of existence. His teachings remain a cornerstone of Hindu philosophy, clear and practical for anyone seeking truth.
