The Bhagwad Gita

An exploration by Shivani Mathur
The Bhagwad Gita is a discourse from Shri Krishna to Arjuna, delivered moments before the greatest battle of his life.

Lecture 1 — Moksha

The Bhagavad Gita, believe it or not, is actually a beginner’s text.

Before I begin speaking directly about the Gita itself, I want to start with something more fundamental — a way of living that is consistent with the principles described in the Vedas.

The word Veda simply means knowledge. The Vedas are a vast body of knowledge that guide a human being toward the highest goal of life — Moksha.

Moksha and Fullness

Moksha is often translated as liberation or freedom. But its deeper meaning is something more subtle.
Moksha is the recognition that you are already complete.
Your peace, happiness, and fulfillment are not fundamentally dependent on external circumstances.

In other words:

  • A Ferrari will not give you lasting happiness.
  • Nor will your favourite food.
  • Not even your favourite person.

Why?
Because you are already full and complete.
Something that is already complete cannot be made more complete by external additions. Objects, achievements, or relationships cannot increase your inner fullness.

What they can do is temporarily quiet the mind or create intense focus. And when the mind becomes quiet — or intensely focused — your natural completeness becomes visible.

In those moments, the constant agitation of the mind subsides, and what has always been present within you briefly reveals itself.

But we often make a mistake.
We assume that the sense of happiness came because of the object, the success, or the person.

Vedanta points out that what we actually experienced was contentment in the presence of something, not contentment because of it.
The fullness was always yours. The external situation simply created the conditions in which it briefly became visible.
Moksha is the recognition of this truth.

Isha Upanishad on Fullness

This idea is beautifully expressed in the Isha Upanishad:

“Pūrṇam adaḥ pūrṇam idaṁ,
pūrṇāt pūrṇam udacyate.”

“That is full. This is full.
From fullness this fullness arises.
Even when fullness emerges from fullness, fullness alone remains.”

In Vedantic language, this verse carries a precise meaning.
Let us introduce some terminology:

  • The phrase “That is full” refers to the universe — the totality of existence. In Vedanta, that fullness is called Brahman, the limitless reality that pervades and sustains the entire cosmos.
  • The phrase “This is full” refers to the individual — you, me, and every living being. The fullness that is the essence of the individual is called Ātman.

The verse is therefore making a profound statement:
The fullness of the individual — Ātman — and the fullness of the universe — Brahman — are not fundamentally different.

Think of it like money: a £5 note in your wallet and a £5 note held by the Bank of England — or even the king — are the same in value, even though they appear in different places and in different hands.

Similarly, the essence of the individual and the essence of the universe are identical, even though they appear as many separate beings and forms.

Empowerment

This is why Vedanta is profoundly empowering.
Its aim is not to diminish the individual, as if we are a small fragment of the universe.
Rather, it reveals that each individual is a complete expression of the same reality that pervades the cosmos.

The essence that sustains the universe — Brahman — is fully present in the individual — Ātman.
In other words, the power of the universe is reflected in each of us, and recognizing this is the essence of self-knowledge.

Preparation and Adhikārī

However, Vedanta is also very honest about something:
This recognition does not arise automatically.
It requires a prepared mind.

Moksha is therefore not a goal that requires effort to reach. It is not something we create or acquire. It already exists within us.
What is required is knowledge.

As traditional Vedantic teachers beautifully say:
You do not become free.
You discover that you were never bound.

However, while Moksha itself requires knowledge rather than effort, a great deal of effort is required in preparing the mind to receive that knowledge.
Vedanta therefore spends a great deal of time explaining not only the goal, but also the qualifications required to understand the teaching.

If Moksha is the top of the ladder, the Bhagavad Gita shows us how to climb that ladder.

In many ways, the Gita is a practical guide to living.
It explains how we must think, act, and live in order to prepare the mind for self-knowledge.

The Context of the Bhagavad Gita

Lecture 2 — Adhikārī

In our previous lecture, we talked about Moksha - the highest teachi g if Vedanta.
we discussed that Moksha is not a goal that requires effort to reach.
It is not something we create or acquire. It already exists within us.
What is required is knowledge that Moksha is our default state.

As traditional Vedantic teachers beautifully say:
You do not become free.
You discover that you were never bound.

However, while Moksha itself requires knowledge rather than effort, a great deal of effort is required in preparing the mind to receive that knowledge.
Vedanta therefore spends a great deal of time explaining not only the goal, but also the qualifications required to understand the teaching.

If Moksha is the top of the ladder, the Bhagavad Gita shows us how to climb that ladder.
It explains how we must think, act, and live in order to prepare the mind for self-knowledge.
It also discusses how some actions need preparation, while others do not.

For example:

  • You need to know algebra before you can learn calculus.
  • You need a strong foundation to build a skyscraper.
  • But you do not need to learn physics to understand history.

Similarly, the recognition of Moksha requires preparation — some steps are sequential, others are parallel processes that do not require pre-training.

Paths toward Moksha

The Bhagavad Gita describes three main paths:

1. Karma Yoga — disciplined action

Karma Yoga cultivates the qualities necessary for understanding Moksha:

  • Example: Starting a business requires enormous effort at first — building systems, solving problems, creating processes.
  • Once the organisation is stable and the processes are streamlined, the same level of effort is no longer required.
  • In the same way, Karma Yoga refines the mind and habits, develops discipline, focus, and perseverance — all preparation steps.

2. Bhakti Yoga — devotion and surrender

Bhakti Yoga is like carrying a heavy bag on a moving train:

  • The train is faster and stronger than you; your own effort alone cannot carry the bag efficiently.
  • But if you surrender to the train, letting it carry the bag while you stay aligned, the journey becomes effortless.
  • Bhakti cultivates trust, devotion, and alignment with a higher power, preparing the mind for Jnana, but it does not itself lead to Moksha.

3. Jnana Yoga — knowledge and inquiry

Only through Jnana can one realize the inherent completeness that is already present:

  • Karma and Bhakti prepare the mind.
  • Jnana is the direct path to understanding that you were never bound.

A person who develops the qualifications for this understanding is called an Adhikārī — someone who has gained the preparedness to pursue this knowledge.
This preparedness has nothing to do with birth, class, or status.
It comes from effort, maturity, and inner development.

Today, we focus on five such qualities — let’s call them our “five a day”:

  • Clarity of purpose
  • Total engagement — a one-pointed mind (ekāgratā)
  • Discipline
  • Excellence in action
  • Detachment

1. Clarity of Purpose

If the ultimate human goal is Moksha — inner freedom — life gains direction.
Responsibilities, work, and relationships are no longer random activities. They become part of a larger process of growth and understanding.

The Bhagavad Gita emphasises that life must be lived through action:
“Perform the work that must be done, for action is superior to inaction.” — Bhagavad Gita 3.8

And the Isha Upanishad echoes a similar idea:
“Doing actions here alone one should wish to live a hundred years.” — Isha Upanishad 2

However, this teaching is meant for someone at the beginning of the journey.
Action in Karma Yoga prepares the mind.
It does not itself lead to Moksha, but it refines and matures the individual, cultivating qualities necessary for understanding.

Think of starting a business:

  • In the beginning, enormous effort is required — systems must be built, problems solved, and processes created.
  • Once the organisation is stable and the processes are streamlined, effort naturally stabilizes.

Karma Yoga works in a similar way — discipline and effort prepare the mind, but the ultimate understanding comes from Jnana.

2. Total Engagement — Ekāgratā

Once purpose becomes clear, the next requirement is focus.
The Gita describes the mind of a person with clarity as one-pointed:
“Those who are resolute in purpose have a single-pointed understanding; the minds of the irresolute branch endlessly in many directions.” — Bhagavad Gita 2.41

Whatever action we undertake, Vedanta asks us to bring our full attention to it.
The Gita illustrates this through a powerful metaphor:
“When one withdraws the senses from sense objects, just as a tortoise withdraws its limbs, that person’s wisdom becomes steady.” — Bhagavad Gita 2.58

This does not mean suppressing the senses.
It means having the ability to direct attention deliberately.
A scattered mind weakens action.
A focused mind strengthens it.

3. Discipline

The third qualification is discipline.
Discipline in the Bhagavad Gita does not mean harsh control or suppression. It means bringing order, balance, and alignment into our lives so that the mind becomes steady and available for deeper understanding.

The Gita first makes an important distinction between external control and true discipline:
“One who restrains the organs of action but continues to dwell mentally on sense objects remains internally unsettled.” — Bhagavad Gita 3.6

In other words, discipline cannot come merely from controlling the body.
True discipline must arise from the mind.
When the mind understands the goal and brings the senses into harmony with that understanding, actions naturally become aligned.

“But the one who controls the senses with the mind and engages the organs of action in the path of action without attachment excels.” — Bhagavad Gita 3.7

Here the principle becomes very clear.
Discipline requires alignment between the mind and the senses.
The mind holds the understanding of our purpose, and the senses participate in actions that support that purpose.
When this alignment exists, behaviour, thought, and intention move in the same direction.
Action then becomes steady, balanced, and purposeful.

The Gita also reminds us that discipline is supported by balance in everyday living:
“Yoga is not for one who eats too much, nor for one who eats too little; not for one who sleeps too much, nor for one who sleeps too little.” — Bhagavad Gita 6.16
“For one who is moderate in food and recreation, balanced in work and rest, and regulated in sleep and wakefulness, yoga becomes the destroyer of suffering.” — Bhagavad Gita 6.17

Discipline therefore begins with moderation and intelligent regulation of how we live.

Discipline has three dimensions:

  • Body: “Reverence for the divine, for teachers, and for the wise; purity, straightforwardness, self-control, and non-violence — this is called discipline of the body.” — Bhagavad Gita 17.14
  • Speech: “Speech that does not cause agitation, that is truthful, pleasant, and beneficial, and the regular study of wisdom — this is called discipline of speech.” — Bhagavad Gita 17.15
  • Mind: “Serenity of mind, gentleness, silence, self-control, and purity of intention — this is called discipline of the mind.” — Bhagavad Gita 17.16

When these dimensions of discipline mature, the mind becomes balanced, steady, and capable of sustained focus.

4. Pursuit of Excellence

When discipline matures, action naturally improves in quality.
This leads to the fourth qualification — excellence.
The Gita defines yoga itself as excellence in action:
“Yogaḥ karmasu kauśalam.” — Bhagavad Gita 2.50

Yoga is excellence in action.
Krishna is not teaching Arjuna how to fight better — Arjuna was already an expert warrior.
Krishna is teaching excellence in motivation, understanding, and the spirit behind action.

The Gita introduces yajña (responsible participation in life’s system):

  • “Nourish the forces that sustain the world, and they will nourish you.” — Bhagavad Gita 3.11
  • “Those who enjoy what is given without offering in return live only for themselves.” — Bhagavad Gita 3.12

In modern terms:

  • Give your best to the system that sustains you.
  • Share the benefits that the system gives back.

Excellence is therefore not only personal success.
It is fulfilling our role responsibly within the larger order of life.

5. Detachment

Finally, detachment.
Detachment does not mean avoiding action.
It means not becoming psychologically dependent on the results of action.

“You have a right to action alone, never to its fruits.” — Bhagavad Gita 2.47

You act fully.
You act responsibly.
But you remain inwardly free.

The Gita describes this state beautifully:
“Just as the ocean remains full though many rivers flow into it, the person who remains undisturbed by desires attains peace.” — Bhagavad Gita 2.70

The ocean does not reject the rivers.
But its level remains unchanged.
In the same way, experiences may come and go.
But the inner state remains steady.

When these five qualities mature — clarity of purpose, focus, discipline, excellence, and detachment — the mind becomes ready to recognise the deeper truth that Vedanta points toward.
And then the Gita says:
“For the one who rejoices in the Self, satisfied in the Self alone, there is nothing that must be done.” — Bhagavad Gita 3.17

At that stage, action is no longer required for fulfillment.
Action was preparation.
Preparation for discovering that the completeness we were seeking outside was always present within.

A question that is often asked: Is it possible to follow the teachings of Vedanta and the Upanishads while still living a worldly life?

This is where the Bhagavad Gita steps in. The Bhagavad Gita comprises 700 verses embedded in the Mahabharata.
The Mahabharata is an epic - the longest in the world comprising nearly 100,000 verses.
The story is about a family of kings - the Kauravas and their cousins, the Pandavas .
The conflicts in this family mirror the complexities of our material existence.

  • Injustice and betrayal: A game of dice, rigged by the Kauravas, is used to strip the Pandavas of their kingdom.
  • Power struggle laced with moral dilemmas: Who should ascend the throne? The Pandavas or the Kauravas?
  • Disputes over land: The Kauravas refuse to give the Pandavas land even "the size of the point of a needle", let alone concede the kingdom.
  • Failed negotiations: Despite several rounds of talks, Duryodhana, leader of the Kauravas, refuses to yield. The only option left is war.
  • Violence and dishonour towards women: Draupadi, the wife of the Pandavas, is humiliated and nearly disrobed in a public assembly.
Every human challenge is present and a war is ultimately invoked.
To crown the problems, Arjuna, a Pandava, the central character,the world’s best archer on becomes wishes to withdraw from the war. At the last minute, he refuses to fight: Misplaced compassion paralyses him. Growing up in a close knit family,the same teachers had trained both him and his cousins, the Kauravas. In this battle, several of his mentors are part of the Kaurava army. Arjuna does not want to kill his guru or kin. He rejects a Pyrrhic, blood-stained victory.

The Gita makes no reference to the pre war problems between the two families. It opens at a moment of high tension—just before the war begins.

Sanjay, the narrator, describes the battlefield to the blind king Dhritarashtra, father of the Kauravas. This narration occurs on Day 10 of the war, shortly after Bhishma Pitamah—the most revered elder of the Kaurava army—has fallen.

Sanjay, formerly Bhishma’s charioteer, is now free to return to the palace to recounts the events leading up to the war to the current king Dhritrastra who is fully vested in the outcome of the war, but is unable to see because he is blind. Here, it is noteworthy to mention that Sanjaya has divine sight to see the war in real time, but he has no authority or knowledge. His narration therefore is a verbatim relay of the conversation between Krishna and Arjuna. The integrity of the Vedantic teachings by Krishna are preserved and devoid of any layers of interpretation. The words of "Bhagwan Krishna" speak directly to us through Sanjaya. This is why the Gita is regarded as one of the highest teachings and is awarded sanctity as a text of Vedanta.
Sanjay describes the moment when Arjuna, the Pandava warrior, is overwhelmed by moral conflict and turns to his charioteer, Krishna—an avatar of God—for guidance, just before the first arrow is released.


The first 28 verses of Chapter 1 (out of 47) are devoted to setting the scene. Sanjay describes the warriors on both sides—their lineage, stature, and credentials. The conch shells are blown, signaling the start of war.
Then, amidst this grandeur, Krishna halts Arjuna’s chariot in the middle of the battlefield. It is here, seeing his gurus, his family and his nearest and dearest in the enemy army, that Arjuna falters. Not due to fear or doubt in his strength, but from emotional turmoil.

Krishna applies the teachings of Vedanta to resolve Arjunas crisis.
The Gita guides us—those immersed in the material world—to see that our inner conflicts, like Arjuna's, can be resolved by following the path of Vedanta.
Every one of us is Arjuna.

Most interesting of all, the Gita shows us that the solution to almost every challenge is remarkably consistent. It offers a path to clarity, purpose, and inner strength by helping us see problems through the lens of different layers of reality. At every level, resolution is essential:

  • Personal duty and action: When we are endowed with a privilege or talent, we have a responsibility to act. We are meant to use our gifts to uphold what is right.
  • Justice in society: If we stay silent or inactive in the face of injustice, the very structure of society begins to fall apart.
  • Understanding what lasts: We must learn to distinguish between what is temporary — like the body — and what is eternal — the Atman.
  • Freedom through wisdom: Ultimately, the Gita teaches that our choices shape our journey toward liberation (Moksha). Every decision matters.

Methodology in the Gita follows the methodology of Vedanta.

 The Gita, a sophisticated and widely read Vedantic text, is a masterclass in content, persuasion, and timing. As Arjuna’s charioteer, Krishna must ensure that Arjuna does not retreat—not from cowardice, but from misplaced compassion.
To abandon the battle would be to allow deceit and Adharma to prevail. And with that, the very foundation of Dharma in society would collapse.

It is interesting to note that in the structure of the Gita itself, we see the methodology of Vedanta described in this section.
As a mentor, Krishna imparts wisdom, but the experience, understanding of the teachings, and ultimately, decision-making are left to Arjuna. Arjuna must reach his own determination.
Indeed, the Gita ends (Chapter 18:63) with the verse:
इति ते ज्ञानम् आख्यातम् गुह्याद्गुह्यतरं मया।
विमृश्यैतदशेषेण यथेच्छसि तथा कुरु॥

Thus, I have explained to you this wisdom—more secret than all secrets.
Reflect on it fully, and then do as you wish.

Throughout the Gita, Krishna does not try to solve Arjuna’s dilemma.
He doesn’t deny Arjuna’s fear—that his loved ones will be killed—nor does he offer comfort or promises of a different outcome. He acknowledges the fear as valid and likely to happen just as Arjuna anticipates.

But rather than removing the fear, Krishna offers something far more powerful: a complete shift in perspective.

This shift is not about changing the situation but about helping Arjuna see that the situation itself is not the real problem.

Krishna introduces Arjuna to the different levels of reality we live in, using relatable examples. These teachings reflect the wisdom of the Mandukya Upanishad, which explores the nature of self and consciousness.

Pujya Swami Dayananda beautifully captures this distinction:
“In psychology, there is no solution (to an emotional problem).
In Vedanta, there is no problem (in having an emotional problem).”

Vedanta does not ask us to suppress our emotions or “bear with” suffering as many traditions might.
Instead, it shows why the problem doesn’t have to be solved or removed—because the problem exists only at one level of reality.
indeed , in this case , at the bodily level Arjuna has a problem - 'death of loved ones' .At a societal level, its a solution - justice and at the level of Atman, it is neither a problem , nor a solution because Atman is eternal. You can read more about these levels in this section.

Krishna explains that even if events unfold exactly as Arjuna fears, the suffering will still be temporary, confined to a limited plane of experience.
You are more than the situation.
The situation will change, but you remain unchanged—because you are eternal.

Even if you make the wrong choice, you remain eternal.
But wrong choices bind you to suffering longer.

So, Krishna's message is this:
Act without fear.
Choose with awareness.
Use your freedom to gain true liberation—here and now.



Notable Omissions:

Conspicuously absent from Krishna’s approach is any focus on battle plans, weapons, or enemies. Although the Bhagavad Gita unfolds moments before the war is about to begin, the dialogue avoids military strategy. Krishna does not address Arjuna’s emotional rant about fighting Bhishma, Drona, or other loved ones. He offers no counsel on war tactics, enemy weaknesses, alliances, or treaties.

The focus is on individual duty and ethical conduct. Krishna does not praise Arjuna’s gurus (his opponents), nor does he dwell on personal loss. All emphasis is on self-reliance.

The idea of an "enemy" is redefined. Instead of focusing on external foes, the Gita highlights internal adversaries like desire and anger. There is no attention to war methodology—only to intent, attitude, and the discernment between what is real and what is unreal.

The Gita is a roadmap to Moksha or freedom; and more urgently, a solution to Arjuna’s immediate personal dilemma: by recognizing what is real (the Atman, justice, and righteousness) and what is temporary noise (wars, relationships, desires), one can choose rightly, endure wisely, and act with clarity.

2:1 Three Perspectives on Arjuna’s Dilemma
In the opening scene of the Bhagavad Gita, three individuals observe Arjuna’s inner turmoil, each interpreting it from their unique vantage point.
It is interesting that an objective situation - the meltdown of Arjuna, is interpreted by three people very differently based on their own context and perspective.

Sanjaya’s Perspective:
Sanjaya, the divine narrator, describes Arjuna as overwhelmed —
“Viṣādaḥ kṛipayāviṣṭaḥ” — filled with sorrow.
To him, Arjuna is paralyzed by emotion, unable to act.

Arjuna’s Perspective:
Arjuna himself views his crisis as one of Karuṇā — compassion.
He sees dharma through the lens of emotion, believing it wrong to fight and kill his kin.

Krishna’s Perspective:
Krishna is the harshest of the three. “What is this delusion - Kaśmalam idam - at this critical hour?” He asks.
To Krishna, Arjuna’s problem is not compassion but ignorance — Kasmal is dirt, a veil of delusion covering his understanding.

The first lesson of the Gita is that none of those perspectives are wrong.
Despite that, the Gita is devoted, not to a systematic exploration of each perspective, but instead a deep dive into Krishna’s perspective. Why?
Because although both Arjun’s and Sanjay’s perspective might be reasonable (and might yield small wins), Krishna’s perspective is a roadmap to the path of freedom (moksha).

2:2-3 Krishna’s Diagnosis of Arjuna’s Delusion

Krishna challenges Arjuna:
श्रीभगवानुवाच ।
कुतस्त्वा कश्मलमिदं विषमे समुपस्थितम् ।
अनार्यजुष्टमस्वर्ग्यमकीर्तिकरमर्जुन ॥ २.२॥
क्लैब्यं मा स्म गमः पार्थ नैतत्त्वय्युपपद्यते ।
क्षुद्रं हृदयदौर्बल्यं त्यक्त्वोत्तिष्ठ परन्तप ॥ २.३॥

“From where has this delusion (kaśmalam) come to you at this crucial moment? It is unworthy of an Arya, does not lead to heaven, and brings disgrace, O Arjuna!”
The word kaśmalam here, often translated as "impurity" or "delusion," can be more precisely understood in the Vedantic sense as āvaraṇa — a veil that obscures the true Self (ātman), truth (satya), and duty (dharma).
Krishna’s rebuke is not mere scolding.
It is a diagnosis: Arjuna is covered by layers of ignorance that veil and obstruct him from seeing the truth and therefore following the righteous path.

The Three Veils (Āvaraṇas) in the Gita:
As fire is covered by smoke, a mirror by dust, and an embryo is constricted in the womb, so is knowledge covered by this (this = desire to abandon your duty under the veil of compassion).

1. Smoke and fire:
When smoke veils a fire, the fire has not yet died, momentarily obscured, it can be fanned back to life with relative ease.
In the same way desire (kāma), attraction (raga) and avoidance (dwesha) can be dispelled through self-effort, devotion, and inquiry. [3.39, 4.42, 5.11.6,25, 18.30]

2. Dust Covering a Mirror:
Requires a little more effort to uncover.
When truth is obscured, uninterrupted effort (sadhana), study, and self-discipline is needed. [4.38,6.5,5.27–28,13.11,14.11]

3. Embryo constrained in the womb:
When deep rooted and unconscious conditioning veils the truth, only time, maturation, and patience will resolve a conflict.

2:11 Methodology in Vedanta (2:2-11 chimneys minute 5)
Proving a point is different from understanding truth.
The process to understand truth is threefold. In any order, the following three conditions must be satisfied.
Listen (Read/observe) to a statement (Pratigyan, Shravan)
Reason (Hetu, Manan)
Apply to an example (Dristanta, Niradhyasana)

Conspicuous by absence.
Conspicuous by absence in Krishna’s perspective is his focus beyond the battlefield at a time when the conch has just been blown, heralding the beginning of war.
The Bhagavad Gita unfolds amidst the imminent clash of the Kurukshetra war, with Krishna and Arjuna positioned between two formidable armies.
One might expect their dialogue to center on military strategies or political maneuvers.

Notable Omissions:
Krishna strategically ignores Arjuna’s rant about his opponents, Drona, Bhishma etc.
He does not even comment on it.
Military Strategy and Enemy Analysis: Arjuna does not seek and Krishna doesn’t offer counsel on battle tactics or enemy vulnerabilities.
There is no mention of alliances, treaties, or negotiations.
Krishna cuts any reference to gurus, relatives and loved ones in the opponent army.
The focus remains on individual duty (dharma) and ethical conduct.

Traditional Methodology:
There is no focus on methodology of war, strategy or sequence of action.
All focus is on intent of action and attitude.

Conventional Guru Worship:
The dialogue lacks customary praises for Arjuna’s gurus (who all happen to be his opponents).
All focus is on self reliance.

Personal Vendettas or Hatred:
The concept of the enemy is redefined, highlighting internal adversaries like desire and anger within oneself rather than external foes or the people/opponents on the battlefield.

In a nutshell all focus is on intent/attitude and discernment between what is real and what is unreal.

Gita is a roadmap for the path to freedom (Moksha) and more immediately it is a roadmap to solve Arjuna’s immediate conflict:
By discerning what is real (ātman, justice and righteousness) and will outlive vs what is temporary noise (wars, relationships, desires) and therefore can be ignored/endured.

Verses 11 - 25 of chapter 2 are dedicated to drawing Arjun’s attention to the u changing nature of the Atman. न त्वेवाहं जातु नासं न त्वं नेमे जनाधिपाः । न चैव न भविष्यामः सर्वे वयमतः परम् ॥ 2-12॥ Translation: “It is not that at any time (in the past), indeed, was I not, nor were you, nor these rulers of men. Nor shall we ever cease to be hereafter.” (Here, Krishna is referring to the Atman.) नासतो विद्यते भावो नाभावो विद्यते सतः। उभयोरपि दृष्टोऽन्तस्त्वनयोस्तत्त्वदर्शिभिः॥ 2:16 In this verse, Krishna distinguishes between the real (Atman) and the apparent (body). To understand the nuance of Krishna’s teachings, it is key to understand the definition of what is “sat” or real. Shankaracharya says that “real” has the quality of Trikāla Abādhitam—that which is non-negatable in the past, present, and future. That which: • Has no beginning (unborn, uncreated) • Has no end (does not die) • Does not change (for change implies destruction and rebirth) What changes (our body) is not truly real. It is only apparently real. Krishna points out to Arjuna that this truth has been clearly seen by the seers (Rishis): “य एनं वेत्ति हन्तारं यश्चैनं मन्यते हतम्। उभौ तौ न विजानीतो नायं हन्ति न हन्यते॥” 2:19 One-way Street: Krishna tells Arjuna that the apparent reality (body, world, actions) cannot affect absolute reality (the Self), but the absolute reality can (and does) affect the apparent. “वेत्त्येनं नित्यमव्ययम् अजं न हन्यते हन्यमाने शरीरे। कथं स पुरुषः पार्थ कं घातयति हन्ति कम्॥” 2:21 Translation: If your true self, my true self, and our collective true self is the Atman—indestructible, eternal, unborn, and inexhaustible—how can the Atman slay or be slain? So when the body is slain, the Atman remains ‘as is.’ He further elaborates: नैनं छिन्दन्ति शस्त्राणि नैनं दहति पावकः । न चैनं क्लेदयन्त्यापो न शोषयति मारुतः ॥ 2-23॥ Translation: “Weapons cannot cleave it, fire cannot burn it, water cannot moisten it, and wind cannot dry it.” These are elements of the material world, therefore they do not affect the atman. Once again, Krishna urges Arjuna to fight—for the right cause, and without hesitation. On the level of apparent reality, where society and relationships exist, his family may perish—but for the sake of justice. Yet on the level of absolute reality, he neither kills nor is killed. The Atman—the true Self—is eternal, untouched by the death of the body. Chapter 25-30 . Why is it his duty?”so what if the Atma dies not die? Why even kill the body ? It’s an excellent question. It’s not enough to say that Arjuna should fight because the Atman and subtle body are eternal and unaffected by death. That may be true on the level of absolute reality, but Krishna’s guidance is not limited to that. Arjuna must fight to uphold dharma—to stand for justice within the context of the material world, the first level of reality. In this transactional reality, where society functions, the fabric of life is sustained by principles—fairness, justice, and ethical conduct. These are what we call Dharma. The word Dharma comes from the Sanskrit root dhri, meaning to hold or uphold. Dharma, then, is what holds society together—it binds individuals to a shared order of responsibility and truth. When these principles are compromised, those who recognize the injustice must act—not because the eternal Self is harmed, but because a failure to act allows rot to grow unchecked in the systems we live within. This message is not theoretical for me. For those who know me, I worked as a minority woman in the male-dominated industry of investment banking. While I was largely treated with respect, there were moments when I experienced clear discrimination. It was not easy to take a stand—especially against a large corporation with more resources, time, and influence in the media than I could ever hope to match. But I chose to speak up. Because when injustice is left unchallenged, it doesn’t disappear—it festers. In those moments, reading the Gita was a strong source of inner strength. Krishna’s words, his call to act in alignment with Dharma—even when the odds are stacked against you—gave me clarity and courage. This is what Krishna is asking of Arjuna—not action from ego or emotion, but right action, grounded in duty and truth, for the sake of maintaining what holds our shared world together. As the Gita progresses, this is precisely what Shri Krishna does .
