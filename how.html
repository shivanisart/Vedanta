Vedanta

Three Words



Vedanta is an ancient Indian framework, a practical methodology for acquiring knowledge. The word ved itself means “knowledge”—not ordinary knowledge, but the most intimate kind: knowledge of the Self. The purpose of history is to understand the past, and the purpose of science is to explore the universe. But the purpose of Vedanta is not to understand the content of the Vedas, but the one who studies them—the Self. Vedanta is not about learning more about our body or even our mind.It is about knowing the very core of our being—directly, immediately. You might think that knowing the Self should be simple. After all, the Self is what’s closest to us. And yet, just as we may miss the forest for the trees, our true nature often escapes us precisely because it is so close, so ever-present. That’s why Vedanta offers not just ideas, but a methodology—a structured means of inquiry to reveal what is already true. In that sense, it is a discovery.
And when this knowledge arises, it’s not abstract. It has real consequences: it transforms how we see the world, how we make decisions, how we relate to others, and how we live—right here, right now.

They say the essence of all the Vedas can be distilled into just three words:

Tat Tvam Asi

Chandogya Upanishad, Chapter 6, Verses 8.8–15

Translated: You are that

What does it mean?

“The essence that gives you (tvam) life is the same infinite power (tat) that animates the entire cosmos.” The words are simple; the implications are far-reaching. If the essence in us, our "self" is the same as the essence that creates everything in the universe,
then:
It means that the power and potential to create the entire universe lies within us.
Clearly very empowering. Then why is there pain and inadequacy in the first place?
How can we stop our suffering? What knowledge will alleviate this?

The Methodology of Vedanta


Vedanta has a goal—to gain self-knowledge. It teaches us how to think, not what to think.

When I say the word “reality,” what comes to your mind?
Maybe it’s your current thoughts, the weather, or your surrounding or even what you feel.  But what about your dream from last night—wasn’t that also “real” while it lasted? Or what about a hallucination, or a memory?

Which one of these is “reality”?
VEDANT says - every one of them is real . But some realities are temporary, some permanent. Every one of us operates at multiple levels of reality simultaneously.  That is why, in  Vedanta, we often explore reality in layers—not just the surface facts of what’s happening, but also what lies underneath. And we shift our perspective so that temporary realities do no perturb us .  To do this, we first have to look at how the Rishis, or ancient seers, saw the world.


×

The Rishis asked a powerful question: Is there something that never changes, even when everything else does? They noticed that: Our bodies change constantly—growing, aging, healing.   Our minds shift—happy one moment, worried the next.   Even the world around us transforms—weather, politics, economies, relationships.   But if everything changes, what holds it all together? What is the unchanging essence that runs through all of it? That’s what they set out to discover. Through intense inquiry and self-exploration, they concluded that yes—there is a changeless essence, and it’s not “somewhere out there.” It’s within us. That unchanging essence is called Brahman.

×

The Rishis described four states of existence - levels of realiity  sthoola Shareera : our physical body (), or the material self which takes input from the five senses - touch smell sight hearing and taste. At this level a human being is motivated by pleasure and pain   sukshma shareera: our subtle body including mind and emotions— The two states are ever-changing and in flux. These are apparent realities. They have a deep and interconnected existence. Our mind/ emotion/ motivation affects the body and vice versa .  The third level is Sushupti—a state of being, rich with potential. It is an unmanifest reality. One way to think of it is that it is a flower waiting to blossom. It is the person we can become, but are not yet.   Underlying these lies the Atman: a steady, constant, timeless, and unchanging reality. This Atman is the common thread running through everything in the universe. The collective of all is called Brahman—and we are that. “Tat Twam  Asi “The Rishi’s  found that beneath the ever-changing material world lies a substratum of unchanging, infinite, indivisible reality—they called it Brahman: the Absolute reality.  This is the unchanging, all-pervasive, timeless truth. The Self, or Brahman.   Everything else is like waves on the ocean—temporary forms of something deeper. Each person, however, experiences their own version or perspective of Brahman ,  this ultimate reality,  at a time. influenced by our unique perceptions and experiences. This means that while we all perceive the same underlying reality, each of us experiences and understands it in our own distinct way. By acknowledging this, Vedanta advocates tolerance for the perspectives of others. 

×

Interestingly, modern physics echoes some of these ideas. At the level of quantum mechanics, matter seems less “solid” than we think—it’s mostly empty space. Particles pop in and out of existence; observation changes outcomes. The deeper scientists look, the more reality seems to blur. In this sense, science and Vedanta are not at odds. Science describes how the universe works. Vedanta asks who is experiencing it.

×

Think of your mind and body as a computer: Your brain is the hardware.   Your thoughts and emotions are like software.   But you—your essential Self—are the electricity that powers it all.   The programs may crash. The hardware might age. But the electricity? It’s untouched, unaffected. Vedanta tells us: You are not the computer. You are the power that enables it. When we mistake ourselves for the software (emotions) or hardware (body), we suffer. When we recognize ourselves as the unchanging essence, we are free.

×

The spiritual journey is not one you take alone. The role of a Guru (spiritual teacher) is considered vital in Vedanta. Why? Because the truth we’re seeking is subtle. It’s not something the eyes can see or the mind can grasp easily. We’re looking for the seer—but we are also the seer! So how do you” see”  the one who sees? This is the subject of the text Drig Dhrushti Vivek.  The Guru is someone who has already seen, someone who can point us back to ourselves. A Guru doesn’t give you a belief system; a true Guru gives you clarity by directing you to find your own evidence . He or she helps remove ignorance, which is like a veil covering the Self. And once that veil lifts, the Self shines on its own. The methods of Vedanta emphasizes: “Shravana” (listening to the teachings), Example “Manana” (reflecting on them), and “Nididhyasana” (deep contemplation and experience . Example  All of these are most effective when guided by a qualified teacher.

×

Vedanta is not an abstract concept , but it’s deeply practical. It’s not just about reading scriptures or chanting mantras. It’s about transformation. This transformation comes from a life of: Essence – Focusing on what really matters.   Discipline – Training the mind and senses, not as punishment, but as preparation.   Focus – Directing your energy toward your deeper nature, not being tossed around by distractions.   When we live like this, a strange thing happens: Suffering becomes optional.  Example. 

×

In Vedanta, meditation isn’t about “feeling good” or “blissing out.” It’s about directly experiencing the Self. It begins by quieting the mind—not forcing it, but observing it. When thoughts settle, what remains? You. Not the “you” with an identity or a to-do list, but the pure, unchanging awareness. That stillness isn’t something you create; it’s something you uncover. The Bhagavad Gita says: “When the mind becomes still, the Self reveals itself.”

×

Vedanta doesn’t ask you to reject the world. You don’t have to live in a cave to be spiritual. In fact, Vedanta teaches that everything in the material world is also Brahman, just temporarily shaped. Your body, your job, your relationships—they’re not separate from the Self. They’re expressions of it. But we suffer when we cling to these expressions as if they’re permanent. We identify with our roles, our past, our emotions. The trick is not to reject the world, but to see through it. Like realizing a movie is just light on a screen—beautiful, emotional, but not ultimately real.

×

One of the foundational ideas in Vedanta is samsara—the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth. Why does this happen? Because of karma—our actions, desires, and attachments. As long as we identify with the ego, we keep coming back, trying to fulfill unfinished business. But there is a way out: It’s called moksha, or liberation. Moksha doesn’t mean going somewhere else. It means waking up to what you already are—free, complete, infinite. Once you realize that, there’s no need to return. Not because you’re escaping, but because you’ve understood the dream

Vedanta is not a religion, not a dogma, and not a philosophy It’s a mirror—held up to the seeker—so they can see who they’ve always been.It doesn’t tell you what to believe.It invites you to look within. In a world chasing identity, status, and external success, Vedanta gently whispers: “You are not what you think. You are far more.” It’s not an escape from the world but a way to truly engage with it—with wisdom, detachment, and compassion. The journey doesn’t end in a mountaintop or monastery. It ends (and begins) in the laser faces  that you give to  your own being. And in the stillness that emerges from the focus, everything is found, here and now.

Sujata:
Mango Motifs and More – Myriad Memories of Kalpana Aunty
When I was about seven, I stayed with my uncle and aunt in Yamunanagar for a few days. One night, we were whisked to the pantry for dinner and told firmly to go to bed after we had finished eating, for there was a party on that evening. Children were not to be seen or heard. My brothers went off to bed, but I couldn’t resist tiptoeing out and peering through a chink in the curtains, to see what all the fuss was about. It was a marvellous scene, with drinks and delicious little snacks and the air was filled with the sound of all kinds of voices. As my eyes scanned the details, they suddenly stopped and focused on what seemed to me to be the loveliest lady I had ever seen. She was seated not too far from the curtain, elegantly draped in a saree and she wore a yellow rose in her hair. Suddenly she looked to her side and spotted me through the chink in the curtain. She gave a big smile and beckoned. I was unsure. Many rules would be broken if I ventured forth, but I couldn’t resist. I stepped out hesitantly and went up to her. She was like a beam of sunshine, and I immensely enjoyed talking to her (as it turned out, I wasn’t admonished either). That was my first meeting with Kalpana Aunty, and each time we meet, it is as though we are swept back to a time when life unfolded in a simple, joyous way, with time enough to do everything and more. That is how I think about her always. Some years later, we all stayed together for several months in Delhi in my grandfather’s house. I remember returning from school every day in time to watch Kalpana Aunty making and handing out perfect paans. The air was always full of conversation, sometimes there was a new game to play or a new thought to ponder over. Life was never still with her around. Soon it was time for the Mathurs to move to their house near Chanakyapuri. I could see that they were greatly looking forward to settling into their home, but I missed the whole family. However, Kalpana Aunty had left behind a few memories that were special for me. Her suddenly ushering me into an old wooden chair and giving me a wonderful new haircut. Measuring me for size and stitching some clothes that fit me really well (I held onto these long after I outgrew them because they were so perfectly made). Sitting with my palm upturned, while she decided on a mehndi pattern to draw out. “She will make a mango,” predicted Shivani. “That’s her favourite.” And indeed, she did. I knew school rules forbade mehndi but I didn’t care. It looked so beautiful! As it turned out, I wasn’t admonished for this either. Her sitting behind me at a late-night puppet show and periodically passing me some delectable mutton sandwiches she had made from dinner leftovers. Many years passed and I would meet her briefly, from time to time. I always wondered if I should spend a summer with her sometime and learn how to stitch my own clothes, make many of the delicacies that I had enjoyed eating at her house, and just watch her effortlessly creating mango motifs and other beautiful art. But it never happened. There now is a mango tree outside my bedroom window. Sometimes, in the early afternoon, when the light is yellow gold and its dark green leaves rustle gently in the breeze, I think of her, and send her love and happiness. Wishing you many happy birthdays ahead, Kalpana Aunty! ​​​​​​​​​​Sujata Varadarajan

Vandana Pant

Kalpana Aunty, The legacy of a teacher’s contributions lies in the unique opportunity to live on in the minds of endless lives touched by 60 minute increments of interacting, learning and integrating the knowledge, perspective and approach put forth by that person in a classroom. The legacy of a mother’s life touches the friends of children as they traipse through homes, finding in the environments of their peers the personality of their parents in every corner - the decor, meals, activities that make up cherished everyday moments. I happen to have wonderful memories of both associations with you. Home Science in school, a dynamic project on marketing research that required wandering through random stores in Hazratganj gathering information on blenders, ovens and home appliances to determine differences in features and prices and availability. A project that inspired the geek in me for a lifetime and I’ve never stopped researching endlessly before any appliance or equipment purchase much to the chagrin of everyone in my family. But my most special memories are of being welcomed to spend endless hours and days at your home in Lucknow and later Delhi. Homes that carried an aesthetic that I always admired; meals that were so creative, and an environment that always felt both modern in thought and charming in its cultural traditions. There was also always your grace that was unmistakably woven into every detail. Marshmallows by the fireplace in your bedroom, Diwali rangoli in the hallway and verandahs, and for some reason my favorite - a cream colored stone statuette adjacent to a vibrant, large plant in the living room. I also remember with clarity, your very firm and non-negotiable “No’s” on certain asks for Nainoo to step beyond boundaries you had set. Tough lessons for a headstrong teenager (me, not her)! Having met you last year, some 40+ years later, your presence rings just the same and it’s so wonderful to have this opportunity to wish you a joyous, special birthday that celebrates who you are. Happy 80th! Vandana
