The Context of the Bhagavad Gita

A question that is often asked: Is it possible to follow the teachings of Vedanta and the Upanishads while still living a worldly life?

This is where the Bhagavad Gita steps in. The Bhagavad Gita comprises 700 verses embedded in the Mahabharata.
The Mahabharata is an epic - the longest in the world comprising nearly 100,000 verses.
The story is about a family of kings - the Kauravas and their cousins, the Pandavas .
The conflicts in this family mirror the complexities of our material existence.

  • Injustice and betrayal: A game of dice, rigged by the Kauravas, is used to strip the Pandavas of their kingdom.
  • Power struggle laced with moral dilemmas: Who should ascend the throne? The Pandavas or the Kauravas?
  • Disputes over land: The Kauravas refuse to give the Pandavas land even "the size of the point of a needle", let alone concede the kingdom.
  • Failed negotiations: Despite several rounds of talks, Duryodhana, leader of the Kauravas, refuses to yield. The only option left is war.
  • Violence and dishonour towards women: Draupadi, the wife of the Pandavas, is humiliated and nearly disrobed in a public assembly.
Every human challenge is present and a war is ultimately invoked.
To crown the problems, Arjuna, a Pandava, the central character,the world‚Äôs best archer wants to withdraw from the war at the last minute. Misplaced compassion paralyses him. He grew up in a close knit family,the same teachers had trained both him and his cousins, the Kauravas. In this battle, several of his mentors are part of the enemy camp - the Kaurava army. Arjuna does not want to kill his guru or kin. He rejects a Pyrrhic, blood-stained victory.

The Gita makes no reference to the pre war problems between the two families. It opens at a moment of high tension‚Äîjust before the war begins.

Sanjay, the narrator, describes the battlefield to the blind king Dhritarashtra, father of the Kauravas. This narration occurs on Day 10 of the war, shortly after Bhishma Pitamah‚Äîthe most revered elder of the Kaurava army‚Äîhas fallen.

Sanjay, formerly Bhishma‚Äôs charioteer, is now free to return to the palace to recounts the events leading up to the war to the current king Dhritrastra who is fully vested in the outcome of the war, but is unable to see because he is blind. Here, it is noteworthy to mention that Sanjaya has divine sight to see the war in real time, but he has no authority or knowledge. His narration therefore is a verbatim relay of the conversation between Krishna and Arjuna. The integrity of the Vedantic teachings by Krishna are preserved and devoid of any layers of interpretation. The words of "Bhagwan Krishna" speak directly to us through Sanjaya. This is why the Gita is regarded as one of the highest teachings and is awarded sanctity as a text of Vedanta.
Sanjay describes the moment when Arjuna, the Pandava warrior, is overwhelmed by moral conflict and turns to his charioteer, Krishna‚Äîan avatar of God‚Äîfor guidance, just before the first arrow is released.


The first 28 verses of Chapter 1 (out of 47) are devoted to setting the scene. Sanjay describes the warriors on both sides‚Äîtheir lineage, stature, and credentials. The conch shells are blown, signaling the start of war.
Then, amidst this grandeur, Krishna halts Arjuna‚Äôs chariot in the middle of the battlefield. It is here, seeing his gurus, his family and his nearest and dearest in the enemy army, that Arjuna falters. Not due to fear or doubt in his strength, but from emotional turmoil.

Krishna applies the teachings of Vedanta to resolve Arjunas crisis.
The Gita guides us‚Äîthose immersed in the material world‚Äîto see that our inner conflicts, like Arjuna's, can be resolved by following the path of Vedanta.
Every one of us is Arjuna.

Most interesting of all, the Gita shows us that the solution to almost every challenge is remarkably consistent. It offers a path to clarity, purpose, and inner strength by helping us see problems through the lens of different layers of reality. At every level, resolution is essential:

  • Personal duty and action: When we are endowed with a privilege or talent, we have a responsibility to act. We are meant to use our gifts to uphold what is right.
  • Justice in society: If we stay silent or inactive in the face of injustice, the very structure of society begins to fall apart.
  • Understanding what lasts: We must learn to distinguish between what is temporary ‚Äî like the body ‚Äî and what is eternal ‚Äî the Atman.
  • Freedom through wisdom: Ultimately, the Gita teaches that our choices shape our journey toward liberation (Moksha). Every decision matters.

Methodology in the Gita follows the methodology of Vedanta.

 The Gita, a sophisticated and widely read Vedantic text, is a masterclass in content, persuasion, and timing. As Arjuna‚Äôs charioteer, Krishna must ensure that Arjuna does not retreat‚Äînot from cowardice, but from misplaced compassion.
To abandon the battle would be to allow deceit and Adharma to prevail. And with that, the very foundation of Dharma in society would collapse.

It is interesting to note that in the structure of the Gita itself, we see the methodology of Vedanta described in this section.
As a mentor, Krishna imparts wisdom, but the experience, understanding of the teachings, and ultimately, decision-making are left to Arjuna. Arjuna must reach his own determination.
Indeed, the Gita ends (Chapter 18:63) with the verse:

Throughout the Gita, Krishna does not try to solve Arjuna‚Äôs dilemma.
He doesn‚Äôt deny Arjuna‚Äôs fear‚Äîthat his loved ones will be killed‚Äînor does he offer comfort or promises of a different outcome. He acknowledges the fear as valid and likely to happen just as Arjuna anticipates.

But rather than removing the fear, Krishna offers something far more powerful: a complete shift in perspective.

This shift is not about changing the situation but about helping Arjuna see that the situation itself is not the real problem.

Krishna introduces Arjuna to the different levels of reality we live in, using relatable examples. These teachings reflect the wisdom of the Mandukya Upanishad, which explores the nature of self and consciousness.

Pujya Swami Dayananda beautifully captures this distinction:
‚ÄúIn psychology, there is no solution (to an emotional problem).
In Vedanta, there is no problem (in having an emotional problem).‚Äù

Vedanta does not ask us to suppress our emotions or ‚Äúbear with‚Äù suffering as many traditions might.
Instead, it shows why the problem doesn‚Äôt have to be solved or removed‚Äîbecause the problem exists only at one level of reality.
indeed , in this case , at the bodily level Arjuna has a problem - 'death of loved ones' .At a societal level, its a solution - justice and at the level of Atman, it is neither a problem , nor a solution because Atman is eternal. You can read more about these levels in this section.

Krishna explains that even if events unfold exactly as Arjuna fears, the suffering will still be temporary, confined to a limited plane of experience.
You are more than the situation.
The situation will change, but you remain unchanged‚Äîbecause you are eternal.

Even if you make the wrong choice, you remain eternal.
But wrong choices bind you to suffering longer.

So, Krishna's message is this:
Act without fear.
Choose with awareness.
Use your freedom to gain true liberation‚Äîhere and now.



Notable Omissions:

Conspicuously absent from Krishna‚Äôs approach is any focus on battle plans, weapons, or enemies. Although the Bhagavad Gita unfolds moments before the war is about to begin, the dialogue avoids military strategy. Krishna does not address Arjuna‚Äôs emotional rant about fighting Bhishma, Drona, or other loved ones. He offers no counsel on war tactics, enemy weaknesses, alliances, or treaties.

The focus is on individual duty and ethical conduct. Krishna does not praise Arjuna‚Äôs gurus (his opponents), nor does he dwell on personal loss. All emphasis is on self-reliance.

The idea of an "enemy" is redefined. Instead of focusing on external foes, the Gita highlights internal adversaries like desire and anger. There is no attention to war methodology‚Äîonly to intent, attitude, and the discernment between what is real and what is unreal.

The Gita is a roadmap to Moksha or freedom; and more urgently, a solution to Arjuna‚Äôs immediate personal dilemma: by recognizing what is real (the Atman, justice, and righteousness) and what is temporary noise (wars, relationships, desires), one can choose rightly, endure wisely, and act with clarity.

2:11 Methodology in Vedanta (2:2-11 chimneys minute 5)
Proving a point is different from understanding truth.
The process to understand truth is threefold. In any order, the following three conditions must be satisfied.
Listen (Read/observe) to a statement (Pratigyan, Shravan)
Reason (Hetu, Manan)
Apply to an example (Dristanta, Niradhyasana)

Conspicuous by absence.
Conspicuous by absence in Krishna‚Äôs perspective is his focus beyond the battlefield at a time when the conch has just been blown, heralding the beginning of war.
The Bhagavad Gita unfolds amidst the imminent clash of the Kurukshetra war, with Krishna and Arjuna positioned between two formidable armies.
One might expect their dialogue to center on military strategies or political maneuvers.

Notable Omissions:
Krishna strategically ignores Arjuna‚Äôs rant about his opponents, Drona, Bhishma etc.
He does not even comment on it.
Military Strategy and Enemy Analysis: Arjuna does not seek and Krishna doesn‚Äôt offer counsel on battle tactics or enemy vulnerabilities.
There is no mention of alliances, treaties, or negotiations.
Krishna cuts any reference to gurus, relatives and loved ones in the opponent army.
The focus remains on individual duty (dharma) and ethical conduct.

Traditional Methodology:
There is no focus on methodology of war, strategy or sequence of action.
All focus is on intent of action and attitude.

Conventional Guru Worship:
The dialogue lacks customary praises for Arjuna‚Äôs gurus (who all happen to be his opponents).
All focus is on self reliance.

Personal Vendettas or Hatred:
The concept of the enemy is redefined, highlighting internal adversaries like desire and anger within oneself rather than external foes or the people/opponents on the battlefield.

In a nutshell all focus is on intent/attitude and discernment between what is real and what is unreal.

Gita is a roadmap for the path to freedom (Moksha) and more immediately it is a roadmap to solve Arjuna‚Äôs immediate conflict:
By discerning what is real (ƒÅtman, justice and righteousness) and will outlive vs what is temporary noise (wars, relationships, desires) and therefore can be ignored/endured.
