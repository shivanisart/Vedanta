The Roadmap to Freedom: Sankhya Yoga

“Sankhya Yoga” combines sam (complete), kha (discernment), and yoga (union), meaning complete discernment through uniting the self with the infinite. Sankhya separates the eternal Atman (true self) from the transient ego, body, and desires, while Yoga unites the Atman with Brahman, the infinite reality. By discerning ego from Atman and uniting with Brahman, you live purposefully, free from burdens, and ready to realize your highest potential.
Derived from the Upanishads, where it’s not explicitly named, Sankhya Yoga is a key concept in the Bhagavad Gita, guiding seekers toward moksha—true freedom from life’s limitations.

What about worldly duties? Can you pursue this knowledge amidst responsibilities? The Gita provides four tools to train yourself, fostering trust and discernment to free you from worldly burdens, equipping you with transferable skills to pursue Sankhya Yoga, the path to moksha:

  • Karma Yoga (Shouldering your responsibilities) Karma means action. Yoga means union. Karma Yoga is action united with,and therefore guided by, discernment.It asks:
    What is the right thing to do?
    Can I act without being bound by outcomes?
    Acting with clarity and responsibility, prepares you for inner freedom. When your house is in order, you are free to leave it.
  • Bhakti Yoga (Learning to trust) Bhakti Yoga meaning "union through love" channels devotion and surrender into every action. Each action becomes an offering, swapping control and ego with love and trust, with deeply knowing that you will are and always will be loved and protected. Bhakti is not giving up action, but replacing desire with unwavering trust so you are free to pursue what matters - self knowledge.
  • Jñāna Yoga (Acquiring the skill of discernment)

    Jñāna Yoga unites actions with intellect. It requires developing a rigourous ability to reason, sifting signal from noise, learning to be curious and inquire. This skill when developed in daily action will translate into the process of self discovery: to discern what is real (sat) and what is apparent (mithya) - the path to liberation: Moksha .

  • Dhyāna Yoga (Path of Meditation)

    Here, the mind and body align through disciplined stillness. This cultivates clarity, enabling deeper realization of the Self.

These four tools will not lead to moksha - they are preparatory tools to 1. free you from worldly burdens and 2. provide transferable skills that prepare you to pursue self-knowledge through Sankhya Yoga, the path to true liberation.
