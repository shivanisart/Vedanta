The Roadmap to Freedom: Sankhya Yoga

Sankhya Yoga is primarily detailed in the *Bhagavad Gita*, where Krishna explains it as a path of knowledge and self-realization through understanding the distinction between the self (*Purusha*) and matter (*Prakriti*). The Gita leans in on the Upanishads, like the Katha and Chandogya, which explore the nature of the self and reality, but they don’t explicitly call it "Sankhya Yoga." Sankhya Yoga in the Gita charts a path to moksha, true freedom from life’s limits. “Sankhya Yoga ” is derived from sam ( complete), kha ( explanation/discern ) and yoga ( to unite) means discernment. Sankhya therefore means separating the eternal self, the Atman, from the fleeting—(ego, body and fleeting desires,) and Yoga establishes that your true self, the Atman, is united with Brahman, the infinite reality. By distinguishing ego from Atman (Sankhya) and uniting Atman with Brahman (Yoga), you live with purpose, free from burdens, ready to fulfill your highest potential.

But what if you have worldly duties? Krishna introduces three tools to help train yourself so that you free yourself from responsibilities to pursue the path of Sankhya and have acquired the basic skills of trust and discernment to travel this path

1. Karma Yoga (Shouldering your responsibilities)

Karma means action. Yoga means union. Karma Yoga is action united with,and therefore guided by, discernment.It asks:

- What is the right thing to do?
- Can I act without being bound by outcomes?

Acting with clarity and responsibility, prepares you for inner freedom. When your house is in order, you are free to leave it.

2. Bhakti Yoga (Learning to trust)

Bhakti Yoga meaning "union through love" channels devotion and surrender into every action. Each action becomes an offering, swapping control and ego with love and trust, with deeply knowing that you will are and always will be loved and protected. Bhakti is not giving up action, but replacing desire with unwavering trust.

3. Jñāna Yoga (Acquiring the skill of discernment)

Jñāna Yoga unites actions with intellect. It requires developing a rigourous ability to reason, sifting signal from noise, learning to be curious and inquire. This skill when developed in daily action will translate into the process of self discovery: to discern what is real (sat) and what is apparent (mithya) - the path to liberation: Moksha .

4. Dhyāna Yoga (Path of Meditation)

Here, the mind and body align through disciplined stillness. This cultivates clarity, enabling deeper realization of the Self.
